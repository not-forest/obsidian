## Grammar fundamentals

Korean language has no gender for anything like English. When saying about self, the "I" part is usually omitted.

| Korean | Phonetic | Translation |
| ------ | -------- | ----------- |
| 남자     | namdzia  | boy         |
| 여자     | jodzia   | girl        |
| 아이     | ai       | child       |
| 막았어요   | magasojo | to eat      |
Plural is rarely used and the amount of object/people shall be understood from the context:
- 저는 사고를 사요 -  I am buying an apple/apples (dzionym saglaryl sajo);

One can accent on plural form (but it is usually not needed) by adding "들" to the end of the word:
- 저는 친구를 만나요 - i am meeting with acquaintance/acquaintances;
- 저는 친구들을 만나요 - i am meeting with acquaintances;

Context is very important in Korean language, because it defines amount, gender etc. The below sentence may have a lot of different translations depending on the context:

| 집에 가요 | I am going home     |
| ----- | ------------------- |
|       | He/she/it goes home |
|       | You are going home  |
|       | Come home!          |
Sentence order is different from English one:

| I am | eating   | an apple | Eng |
| ---- | -------- | -------- | --- |
| I am | an apple | eating   | Kor |
Both verbs and adjectives can be changed based on tense:

| to go | 가다  | to be hungry | 배고프다  |
| ----- | --- | ------------ | ----- |
| went  | 갔어요 | was hungry   | 배고팠어요 |
### Agglutination

Korean language is agglutinational, which means that words grow with additional endings, each providing some additional meaning to the word:

| verb | if  | and | or  |
| ---- | --- | --- | --- |
| 가다   | 다면  | 가고  | 가지만 |
Words do not deffirentiate based on article case, but rather obtain additional ending:

| Article Case | English | Korean |
| ------------ | ------- | ------ |
| Nominative   | girl    | 여자*가*  |
| Accusative   | girl    | 여자*를*  |
| Genitive     | girl    | 여자*의*  |

### Politeness

Korean language has a lot of ways to be polite, which is made by using special additions between words. 

We separate *polite language (존댓말, dziondenmal)* - usually used in official meetings and situations with people we respect or random people. Other type of language is *familiar language (반말, panmal)* - usually used with people we know and where there is no need for formality.

Formality is being shows in different ways:
1. Based to whom we talk:
	- To friend: 
	나는 학교에 가 - I am going to school.
	- To unfamiliar person
	자는 학교에 가*요* - I am going to school.
	- To teacher:
	자는 학교에 *갑니다* - I am going to school.
2. Based about whom we talk:
	- About friend:
	친구*가 집*에 *있어요* - A friend is at home.
	- About grandma:
	할머니*께서* *댁*에 *계세요*. - A grandma is home.
3. Based on who is the receiver of what we talk. (later)

Besides endings for different formalities, some words even have their own formal and neutral forms:

| Neutral Form         | Formal Form                    |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| 나 - I                | 저 - I (formal)                 |
| 집 - home             | 댁 - home (formal)              |
| 있다 - to be somewhere | 계시다 - to be somewhere (formal) |
